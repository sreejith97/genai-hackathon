1. Presenting Complaint

The patient presents with an 8-month history of persistent fatigue, unexplained weight gain of approximately 7 kg, significant hair thinning and hair loss on the scalp, cold intolerance, constipation, and a general feeling of "brain fog" affecting her ability to concentrate at work. She reports that her menstrual cycles have become increasingly irregular over the past 5 months. The patient states that her GP initially attributed these symptoms to stress and lifestyle factors and advised rest and dietary changes without ordering any investigations.

7. Assessment & Diagnosis

Primary Diagnosis: Hypothyroidism secondary to Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (Autoimmune Thyroiditis)


The clinical presentation, elevated TSH, suppressed free T4 and T3, markedly elevated Anti-TPO and Anti-Thyroglobulin antibodies, and ultrasound findings are all consistent with a diagnosis of Hashimoto's Thyroiditis — an autoimmune condition in which the immune system attacks the thyroid gland, reducing its ability to produce sufficient thyroid hormone.


Secondary Findings:

  • Iron-deficiency anaemia (recurrent) — likely exacerbated by hypothyroid state
  • Vitamin D deficiency — requires therapeutic supplementation
  • Vitamin B12 borderline low — to be monitored
  • Secondary hypercholesterolaemia — expected to improve with thyroid treatment
8. Management Plan
  • Levothyroxine 50mcg daily, to be titrated. Commence immediately.
  • Thyroid function repeat in 6–8 weeks to reassess TSH and Free T4.
  • Ferrous fumarate 210mg twice daily for iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Cholecalciferol 20,000 IU weekly for 8 weeks, then reassess Vitamin D levels.
  • Vitamin B12 supplementation initiated (1000mcg daily oral).
  • Dietary advice: high-fibre diet for constipation; avoid excessive iodine or raw cruciferous vegetables.
  • Patient counselled on the chronic nature of Hashimoto's thyroiditis and the expected timeline for symptom improvement (6–12 weeks after starting treatment).
  • Referral to dietitian for weight management support.
  • Follow-up appointment in 8 weeks. If TSH remains elevated, increase Levothyroxine to 75mcg.
9. Clinician's Notes

Patient was understandably frustrated that these symptoms — which had significantly impacted her quality of life for over 8 months — were not investigated by her GP despite multiple presentations. She should have been referred for thyroid function tests at her second or third visit. The elevated antibodies suggest this condition has likely been developing over a prolonged period. Prognosis with appropriate thyroid hormone replacement is excellent. Patient is aware.
